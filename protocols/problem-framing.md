# Problem Framing Protocol

Problem framing prevents teams from building solutions before they understand the job, constraint, buyer, and urgency.

## When To Load

Load for product strategy, PRDs, UX research, roadmap decisions, founder due diligence, feature prioritization, and any request that starts with a proposed solution.

## Required Inputs

- Proposed objective or solution
- Target user or buyer
- Current alternative
- Trigger event
- Pain evidence
- Business outcome desired
- Constraints

## Method

1. Restate the problem without the proposed solution.
2. Identify the user, buyer, and decision-maker.
3. Identify the trigger event that makes the problem urgent now.
4. Describe the current alternative.
5. Separate:
   - facts
   - inferences
   - assumptions
6. Define success from the user's perspective.
7. Define business success.
8. List what would falsify the problem.
9. Decide whether to discover, prototype, sell, or stop.

## Decision Gates

- No specific user: continue discovery.
- No current alternative: pain may be imaginary.
- No trigger event: urgency is weak.
- No willingness to pay or change behavior: do not build yet.
- Founder preference without buyer evidence: mark as assumption.

## Output Format

```markdown
# Problem Frame - [topic]

## User / Buyer
## Trigger Event
## Current Alternative
## Pain Evidence
## Assumptions
## Falsification Test
## Decision
```

## Failure Modes

- Solution Disguise: feature request presented as problem.
- Founder Projection: founder pain assumed to be market pain.
- Persona Fog: "small businesses" or "creators" without a real operating context.
- Urgency Gap: problem exists but can wait forever.
